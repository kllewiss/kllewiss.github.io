Sharity is an app that aims to support non-profit organizations (NPOs) and those who are seeking them out by connecting the two within their communities. This allows people to support the causes they believe in while resolving NPOs' struggles with visibility and encouraging donations.

Project Type

Product Design (user research, planning, design, testing)

Project Length

3 months

Tools Used

Typeform Survey, Figma, Invision

The Problems

01.People need easier ways to find donation centers

2019 was the year of tidying up. As more people are seeking to organize and give away the things that no longer spark joy in their lives, the desire for ethical rehoming of belongings has risen.

The problem occurs when you go online and search for "places to donate clothes." Search engines tend to favor for-profit locations, however, as I learned through my research, many people would rather support their local non-profit organizations, as they trust that their practices are kind to the environment, humanity, and can directly benefit their local community.
‍

02.People don't know where & how to donate

According to a study done by industry experts in User Experience Research, the Nielsen Norman Group, users of non-profit websites have reported significant difficulty finding information regarding non-monetary donations.
‍
The result of this problem is that users must spend time reviewing several non-profits before they can make an educated decision. However, we know from studies involving people and food choices that ultimately, we favor convenience over all else, so the user may opt for a more convenient solution, such as putting the items in a landfill or taking items to a for-profit center with easy drop-off protocols.

User Research

To begin the design process, I reached out to understand my potential users and their concerns.

I created a survey to get ideas and input from potential users. I gathered demographic information, and then asked about donation habits: how often they donate, what they donate, and where they prefer to donate, as well as concerns they had when donating.

Worry about convenience

Half of the survey respondents were worried about the inconvenience of donation drop-offs

Can’t find information online

The overwhelming majority of respondents expressed frustration over the lack of information online

Question the trustworthiness

Some users want to ensure the items would go to good use rather than end up in a landfill

The Competition

I conducted an analysis of the main competition, Google Maps.

Many people will rely on the framework and product that they are already comfortable and familiar with: Google and/or Google Maps. I went through the process that I imagined our potential user would carry out to search for a place to donate their gently used clothing. Through this, I learned that if you open Google Maps and search for donations in your area, you will more than likely be inundated with suggestions for thrift shops and Goodwill, which my research participants wanted to avoid.

Pain Points

  • Too many distracting and misleading results, such as for-profit donation centers (Goodwill, thrift shops, etc.) and also retail stores which don't fit the inquiry at all (Target, Baby Depot, etc.)
  • The user must take extra steps to achieve their goal: find a non-profit in the results, verify it's legitimacy and status, then visit it's website to gather information and/or call to find out the logistics of how, when, and what they can donate

The Mockups

I created low-fidelity mockups to test design ideas.

After sketching, wire-framing, and discussing solutions with my visual designer, we created some mockups for the expected user flow and interface. We knew we needed certain key features: easy access to a map of locations, a way to narrow down search criteria, and additional important information regarding the NPOs.

I made two major adjustments in this phase of the design iteration:

  • Removed bottom toolbar in favor of a simple floating search button, similar to what users are used to seeing in apps like Google Maps and also Twitter
  • Changed the NPO profile page from being a modal to a full page, as there is a lot of interactivity and information, so I expect a lot of time will be spent on those pages

What I Learned

01.Communication with your team is key

For this project, I worked with a graphic designer to create illustrations, icons, and the logo design. A lot of time and energy went into clearly communicating the goals of the product, what we needed, and what we were hoping for throughout the project, but that effort to communicate clearly paid off as they were able to create beautiful assets which fit into the project seamlessly.

02.Deeply consider all possible solutions to the problem

This was simply a project created for the purpose of developing my skills as a designer, but as I began it, creating a mobile application was the only possibility that crossed my mind.
‍
In retrospect, if this were being designed as an real-life solution to the problem posed at the beginning, I now think that a website is better suited for this project.

Here are some of the reasons why I came to this conclusion:

  • Price
    If this is a service geared towards supporting non-profits, it's imperative that measures to reduce the cost of production are taken. The cost of building and maintenance of a mobile application is far higher than developing a website.
  • USER LOCATION
    If they are spending around 30 minutes researching locations to donate items from their home, they will more than likely want to do so from the larger device, so designing for web would be preferable.
  • EASE OF USE
    ‍    If we were to continue forward with the product as a mobile app, we'd still need a landing page to redirect potential users to download the application. Any chance to get the user to interact with the product as quickly as they can use a competitor, such as Google Maps, would be best.
